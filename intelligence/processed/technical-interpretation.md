## Gemini Multimodal Analysis — Integrated Findings

*Source: 4 Gemini multimodal transcriptions of Core Log Sheet, Map1, Map2, Map3*

### Core Log — Subsurface Mineralization at Depth (DH5-SBF1-A)

**Drill Hole Metadata:**
- **ID**: DH5-SBF1-A | **Core Size**: HQ3 | **Total Depth**: 28.00m
- **Coordinates**: 7° 5' 28.07" N, 126° 14' 4.42" E (Barangay Tagbinonga, Mati)
- **Logged by**: Geol. Medilyn Gonzaga | **Date**: February 25, 2024

**Mineralization Profile by Depth Zone:**

| Zone | Depth (m) | Lithology | Alteration | Mineralization | Interpretation |
|------|-----------|-----------|------------|----------------|----------------|
| **Overburden** | 0–2 | Basalt/Limestone fragments | Mod-highly weathered | None | Soil + weathered rock cover |
| **Upper propylitic** | 2–10 | Basalt | Slightly weathered | 5–15% hematite; manganese; silica veins | Propylitic + silicified; Fe-Mn oxide zone |
| **Transition** | 10–16 | Basalt | Slightly weathered | 10–20% hematite; 20% manganese; silica veins/stringers | Peak hematite-manganese at 12–14m; fern-like Mn dendrites |
| **Oxide zone** | 16–24 | Basalt (aphanitic) | Slightly-mod weathered | **25–40% hematite; 10–25% manganese; 5–10% limonite** | Strongest Fe mineralization — oxidized along fractures |
| **Lower silicified** | 24–28 | Basalt (porphyritic) | Slightly weathered | Silica veins/stringers; epidote | Waning mineralization; argillic alteration at bottom |

**Key findings:**
1. **Peak iron mineralization at 16–20m depth** — 40% hematite + 25% manganese + 10% limonite at 16–17m. This is the highest-grade interval.
2. **Fracture-controlled mineralization** — iron oxides consistently described as "oxidized along fractures only," indicating structurally hosted deposit, not disseminated.
3. **Pervasive fragmentation** — every interval from 2–28m shows "fragmented" to "brecciated" deformation (moderate to high). The rock is intensely fractured throughout, which is favorable for open-pit extraction but raises geotechnical concerns for pit wall stability.
4. **Propylitic-silicified alteration envelope** — epidote, chlorite, silica, calcite fracture fills throughout 2–16m. Classic epithermal/porphyry-proximal alteration assemblage.
5. **No sulfide copper mineralization logged** — despite surface assays showing up to 39.5% Cu, this drill hole records only iron oxide minerals. The copper mineralization may be in a different zone of the deposit (the drill hole is at Fe Ore Point 8/9 area, not the copper zone).
6. **Shallow deposit** — total depth only 28m with mineralization starting at 2m. Very low strip ratio potential for open-pit mining.
7. **Argillic alteration at 27–28m** — clay alteration at the base suggests proximity to a deeper hydrothermal system.

### Geological Setting — Structural Controls on Mineralization (Map2)

**Map Details:**
- **Scale**: 1:7,000 | **Contour interval**: 10m
- **Prepared by**: Medilyn Gonzaga
- **Coverage**: 7°5'–7°6' N, 126°13'30"–126°14' E

**Rock Units Identified:**
| Unit | Description | Distribution |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| **Basalt** | Dark blue; porphyritic to aphanitic; host rock for iron ore | Central-eastern area |
| **Dacite** | Pink; plagiophyric; 5–25m exposures; propylitic | Central ridge trending NW-SE |
| **Metasedimentary** | Yellow-orange/brown; intercalated with iron ore lenses | Northern and western flanks |
| **Limestone** | Green hash; lenses within dacite and metasedimentary | Scattered; effervesces with acid |

**Structural Framework — Three Major Fault Orientations:**

| Fault | Orientation | Location | Significance |
|-------|-------------|----------|-------------|
| **F1** | N43°W / 55°SW | Northwest (near Fe Point 5) | Cuts basalt-andesite; controls NW mineralization |
| **F2** | S76°E / 87°SW | Southeast creek | Near-vertical; controls Fe Points 6–10 cluster |
| **F3** | N26°W / 74°NE | Central dacite-limestone contact | Perpendicular to contact; controls landslides |

**Critical geological interpretation:**
1. **NW-SE trending structural corridor** — all iron ore occurrences align along a NW-SE structural trend that follows the dacite-metasedimentary contact zone. This is the primary ore-controlling structure.
2. **Contact-hosted mineralization** — iron ore occurs preferentially at lithological contacts (metasedimentary-dacite, basalt-dacite), not within a single rock unit. This is consistent with skarn or contact-metasomatic deposit models.
3. **Acid leaching evidence** — multiple annotations note "evidence of acid leaching," indicating supergene enrichment processes have upgraded near-surface iron grades. This explains the very high surface assay grades (53–67% Fe).
4. **Propylitic alteration is pervasive** — epidote, chlorite, silica, pyrite alteration described across all rock units, indicating a large hydrothermal system affected the entire mapped area.
5. **Landslide risk** — localized landslides mapped in the concession area, associated with the highly jointed/fragmented rock mass and steep terrain. This is a geotechnical consideration for mine planning.

## Electromagnetic Resistivity (EM) Survey Data

*Source: 4 images (Emscan1-4.jpeg) from geophysical survey report — 3D geoelectric resistivity, induced polarization (IP), and VLF electromagnetic surveys*

### Survey Methods Deployed

Three complementary geophysical methods were used across the GMC concession:

| Method | What It Measures | Depth Penetration | Key Application |
|--------|-----------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| **Geoelectric Resistivity** | Rock resistance to electrical current | Deep (hundreds of meters) | 3D mapping of ore body geometry |
| **Induced Polarization (IP)** | Chargeability — ability of rock to store electrical charge | Matches resistivity | Detects disseminated sulfide minerals (including Cu sulfides) |
| **VLF Electromagnetic** | Subsurface conductivity along survey lines | 100–200m | Maps conductive structures (faults, shear zones, massive sulfides) |

### 3D Resistivity Volume — Pipe-Like Structure (Figure 10, Emscan1)

The 3D resistivity inversion (3DGEm model) reveals a **pipe-like anomalous structure** initially interpreted as a **diatreme** (volcanic pipe). Key findings:

- **Two distinct resistivity regions** separated by a gap where the anomalous conduit is located
- The conduit is positioned within the **tectonically active volcanic arc** and intersected by **major fault systems** (thrust faults shown as blue lines)
- Interpretation: **deep-seated magmatic-hydrothermal source**, consistent with **porphyry or epithermal mineralization**
- The delineation gap trends **NNW to SSE**, aligning with the dominant structural orientation identified in the geological mapping (Map2 NW-SE fault systems)

### 3D Resistivity Volume — Subsurface Visualization (Emscan3 & Emscan4)

The full 3D resistivity volume renders show:

- **Near-surface (0–80m)**: Highly variable resistivity — multiple discrete anomalies shown as red, magenta, green, and yellow zones. These correspond to the heterogeneous near-surface geology (basalt, dacite, metasedimentary, limestone) and iron oxide mineralization confirmed by drilling and surface mapping.
- **Mid-depth (~80–200m)**: Discrete **conductive bodies** (shown as purple/dark circular anomalies within dominant yellow background). These are the critical targets — conductive zones at depth in a resistivity survey typically indicate **sulfide mineralization** (pyrite, chalcopyrite, bornite) or fluid-filled fracture zones.
- **Deep (>200m)**: Predominantly uniform yellow (moderate resistivity) with **vertical columnar structures** extending to the base of the model. These vertical features may represent the roots of the hydrothermal system — mineralized veins or alteration pipes feeding the near-surface ore bodies.
- **Thrust fault planes** (blue dashed lines in Emscan4 left panel) cut through the volume at moderate angles, consistent with the mapped fault orientations (N43°W, S76°E, N26°W).
- **Cross-sectional view** (Emscan4 right panel) shows depth scale from surface (~1100m elevation) to approximately 400–600m below surface, with distinct anomalous zones visible as concentric green/blue features at mid-depth.

### VLF Electromagnetic Results (Section 3.2, Emscan1)

- **Multiple linear conductive anomalies** detected along survey lines
- Anomalies aligned **NW-SE and NE-SW** — matches the two dominant structural trends from geological mapping
- Fraser and Karous-Hjelt filter analysis confirms anomalies are **structurally controlled** (fault zones and shear zones)
- Interpreted as consistent with **epithermal or porphyry copper-gold system**
- VLF profiles imaged features to **100–200m depth**
- **Intermediate to high resistivity anomalies (800–5,000 ohm-meters)** at approximately **80m below surface**
- Strong correlation between VLF Line 9 and geoelectric resistivity Lines 3 and 4 — **cross-method validation** of the same subsurface features

### Porphyry-Epithermal Deposit Model (Emscan2)

The report includes a **classic Sillitoe-type porphyry-epithermal deposit model** cross-section, showing how the GMC deposit is being interpreted within a well-established geological framework:

| Zone | Depth/Position | Mineralization Style | Metals | GMC Evidence |
|------|---------------|---------------------|--------|-------------|
| **Surface epithermal** | Near-surface | High-sulfidation disseminated | Au ± Ag ± Cu | Surface gold assays (0.2–4.4 g/t) |
| **High-sulfidation lode** | Shallow-intermediate | Vein/lode | Cu-Au ± Ag | High-grade Cu surface samples (39.5%) |
| **Proximal Cu-Au skarn** | Intermediate (marble front) | Contact metasomatic | Cu-Au | Contact-hosted mineralization on Map2 |
| **Porphyry core** | Deep (~1km+) | Disseminated/stockwork | Cu ± Au ± Mo | 3D resistivity pipe-like structure |
| **Subepithermal veins** | Intermediate | Vein | Zn-Cu-Pb-Ag ± Au | Mercury in assays suggests deep source |
| **Carbonate replacement** | Distal | Replacement bodies | Zn-Pb-Ag ± Au (Cu) | Limestone lenses in geological mapping |

The inclusion of molybdenum (Mo) in the MPSA commodity list (molybdenum, copper, gold) is significant — **Mo is a hallmark of porphyry copper deposits**, and the fact that MPSA 251(A) specifically lists it suggests the original geological assessment recognized porphyry potential.

### Critical Assessment: Does EM Data Confirm or Contradict Surface Copper Assays?

**CONFIRMS — with significant caveats.**

Evidence supporting copper at depth:
1. **Pipe-like structure** interpreted as diatreme/hydrothermal conduit — classic feeder for porphyry copper
2. **Conductive anomalies at 80–200m depth** — sulfide minerals (including chalcopyrite) are strong conductors
3. **IP survey correlation** — induced polarization specifically detects disseminated sulfides, and the report states IP anomalies correspond with resistivity anomalies
4. **Structural control matches surface geology** — same NW-SE fault systems control both surface iron ore and subsurface anomalies
5. **Porphyry model fit** — the deposit characteristics (propylitic alteration, iron oxides at surface, Cu-Au-Mo commodity suite, volcanic arc setting) are textbook porphyry copper indicators
6. **VLF interpretation explicitly states** the anomalies are "characteristic of epithermal or porphyry copper-gold system"

Caveats and limitations:
1. **Geophysics does not directly measure copper content** — it measures physical properties (resistivity, chargeability) that are consistent with, but not diagnostic of, copper mineralization
2. **No subsurface copper assay data** — the single drill hole (DH5-SBF1-A, 28m) intersected only iron oxides. No drill hole has tested the conductive anomalies at 80–200m depth
3. **The pipe-like structure interpretation as a "diatreme" needs verification** — diatremes can be barren or mineralized; the interpretation is model-driven, not confirmed by drilling
4. **Report provenance unclear** — page numbers (PAGE 11) suggest this is excerpted from a larger report; the full report with methodology, survey parameters, and complete interpretation is not in the document set
5. **No resource estimate** — EM data can delineate targets but cannot quantify tonnage or grade without drilling

### Integration with Existing Technical Data

The EM survey data fundamentally changes the technical narrative:

**Before EM data**: Surface samples showed high Cu grades (1.3–39.5%) but the only drill hole found no copper, suggesting the Cu and Fe zones were spatially separate. The copper resource was unconfirmed at depth.

**After EM data**: The geophysical surveys identify **conductive anomalies at 80–200m depth** aligned with the same structural controls as surface mineralization. The deposit is now interpretable as a **porphyry copper-gold system** where:
- **Iron oxides at surface** = supergene cap / oxidized zone above the sulfide system
- **High copper grades at surface** = leached and enriched near sulfide outcrops
- **Conductive anomalies at depth** = potential sulfide ore body (the main prize)
- **Pipe-like structure** = hydrothermal feeder conduit

This is a more valuable deposit model than separate Fe and Cu zones. A porphyry copper-gold system in the Philippines, with surface grades of 1.8–39.5% Cu and 0.2–4.4 g/t Au overlying a geophysically defined target at depth, is a **drill-ready exploration target** that would attract significant mining industry interest.

**The critical next step remains the same: drill the geophysical targets.** A program of 5–10 diamond drill holes to 200–400m depth, targeting the conductive anomalies, would confirm or deny the porphyry copper model. If confirmed, this transforms the project from a surface iron ore operation into a multi-commodity porphyry copper-gold-iron-molybdenum development with dramatically higher value.
